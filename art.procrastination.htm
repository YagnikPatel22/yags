How to Stop Procrastinating

A deep dive into Skinner's Law and the pleasure-pain framework that governs human behavior. Learn how to hack your motivation when you find yourself avoiding important work.

Understanding Skinner's Law

In 1789, philosopher Jeremy Bentham mused on the two forces that control our lives: the pursuit of pleasure and the avoidance of pain. This general idea is the basis for the most useful productivity framework you've probably never heard of.

It's called Skinner's Law. The law is named after behavioral psychologist B.F. Skinner, who was a pioneer in the study of how rewards and punishments shape human behavior.

Skinner's Law says that to get yourself to do something, you need to either:

  • Make the pain of not doing it greater than the pain of doing it, or
  • Make the pleasure of doing it greater than the pleasure of not doing it

It's a very simple way of hacking your motivation when you find yourself procrastinating on or avoiding something important.

Practical Application

When I was writing my book, I kept saying it was my top priority, but weeks would pass without meaningful progress. Why? Ultimately, it was because of a pleasure-pain mismatch. Given how far out the deadline was, it was much easier (higher pleasure, lower pain) to skip writing on any given day than it was to sit down and work.

To fix this, I needed to adjust the equation. I made the pain of not writing greater by creating public accountability and setting up daily check-ins with a writing partner. I made the pleasure of writing greater by rewarding myself after each writing session.

"The framework is simple, but the impact is profound. Next time you find yourself procrastinating, ask: How can I adjust the pleasure-pain equation?"
