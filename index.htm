How to Stop Procrastinating

A deep dive into Skinner's Law and the pleasure-pain framework that governs human behavior. Learn how to hack your motivation when you find yourself avoiding important work.

Understanding Skinner's Law

In 1789, philosopher Jeremy Bentham mused on the two forces that control our lives: the pursuit of pleasure and the avoidance of pain. This general idea is the basis for the most useful productivity framework you've probably never heard of.

It's called Skinner's Law. The law is named after behavioral psychologist B.F. Skinner, who was a pioneer in the study of how rewards and punishments shape human behavior.

Skinner's Law says that to get yourself to do something, you need to either:

  • Make the pain of not doing it greater than the pain of doing it, or
  • Make the pleasure of doing it greater than the pleasure of not doing it

It's a very simple way of hacking your motivation when you find yourself procrastinating on or avoiding something important.

Practical Application

When I was writing my book, I kept saying it was my top priority, but weeks would pass without meaningful progress. Why? Ultimately, it was because of a pleasure-pain mismatch. Given how far out the deadline was, it was much easier (higher pleasure, lower pain) to skip writing on any given day than it was to sit down and work.

To fix this, I needed to adjust the equation. I made the pain of not writing greater by creating public accountability and setting up daily check-ins with a writing partner. I made the pleasure of writing greater by rewarding myself after each writing session.

The framework is simple, but the impact is profound. Next time you find yourself procrastinating, ask: How can I adjust the pleasure-pain equation?

The 5 Types of Wealth

Money is just one form of wealth. Discover the five types that truly matter and how to build a rich life across all dimensions.

Redefining Wealth

We live in a world that equates wealth with money. But true wealth is measured across multiple dimensions, and financial wealth is just one piece of the puzzle.

The Five Types of Wealth

1. Social Wealth: The strength of your relationships and community. This includes deep friendships, family bonds, and meaningful connections. No amount of money can replace the joy of genuine human connection.

2. Time Wealth: The freedom to spend your time as you choose. It's not about having unlimited time, but having control over how you use it. The wealthiest people guard their time fiercely.

3. Physical Wealth: Your health and vitality. No amount of money can compensate for poor health. Energy, strength, and wellness are the foundation for everything else in life.

4. Mental Wealth: Peace of mind, emotional well-being, and mental clarity. This is the foundation for everything else. Without mental wealth, no other form of wealth truly matters.

5. Financial Wealth: Yes, money matters. But it's a tool to build the other four types of wealth, not an end in itself. Money provides options, security, and freedom‚Äîbut only when used intentionally.

Building True Wealth

The wealthiest people I know aren't necessarily the richest. They're the ones who have achieved balance across all five dimensions. Start tracking your wealth across all five types, and make intentional choices to build each one.

Mental Models

First Principles Thinking

Break down complex problems to their fundamental truths. This is how Elon Musk, Aristotle, and other great thinkers approach challenges.

What Is First Principles Thinking?

First principles thinking is the practice of breaking down complex problems into their most basic, foundational elements‚Äîthe "first principles"‚Äîand rebuilding from there.

Instead of reasoning by analogy (doing things because that's how they've always been done), first principles thinking asks: What are the fundamental truths? What do we know to be absolutely true?

The Elon Musk Example

When Elon Musk wanted to build SpaceX, everyone told him rockets were too expensive. The going rate to buy a rocket was $65 million. Instead of accepting this, he asked: What are rockets made of? What is the raw material cost?

He discovered that the materials cost was only about 2% of the typical rocket price. The rest was markup, inefficiency, and legacy costs. By rebuilding from first principles, SpaceX was able to reduce costs by 10x.

How to Apply It

1. Identify and challenge your assumptions
2. Break the problem down to fundamental truths
3. Create new solutions from the ground up

This approach works for business problems, personal challenges, and strategic decisions. The next time you face a complex problem, resist the urge to reason by analogy. Go back to first principles.

The Stoic Path to Resilience

Ancient Stoic wisdom applied to modern challenges. How to build unshakeable mental strength in an age of constant change.

The Core of Stoicism

Stoicism, developed in ancient Greece and Rome, offers a practical philosophy for navigating life's challenges. At its core is a simple but profound insight: We cannot control what happens to us, but we can always control how we respond.

The Dichotomy of Control

The Stoics divided everything into two categories: things we can control and things we cannot. We can control our thoughts, actions, and responses. We cannot control other people, external events, or outcomes.

Most of our suffering comes from trying to control what we cannot control, and neglecting what we can. The path to resilience is accepting this dichotomy and focusing our energy accordingly.

Three Stoic Practices for Modern Life

Negative Visualization: Regularly imagine losing what you have. This builds gratitude and prepares you for potential loss. By mentally rehearsing loss, you become more resilient when challenges actually arise.

The View from Above: Zoom out and see your problems from a cosmic perspective. Most of what troubles us is small when viewed from this lens. This practice helps you maintain perspective and avoid being overwhelmed by minor setbacks.

Voluntary Discomfort: Deliberately practice discomfort (cold showers, fasting, hard workouts) to build resilience and reduce dependence on comfort. By choosing discomfort, you train yourself to handle involuntary discomfort better.

Building Unshakeable Strength

Stoicism isn't about suppressing emotions‚Äîit's about not being controlled by them. It's about building mental strength that serves you in both good times and bad.
